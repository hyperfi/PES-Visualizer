💡 Nilsson Model Theory

In the spherical shell model, single-particle states are highly degenerate. When a nucleus deforms, this degeneracy is broken. The Nilsson Model describes how these levels split under spheroidal (\(\beta\)) and triaxial (\(\gamma\)) deformation:

\[H = H_0 - \frac{2}{3}\beta \hbar\omega_0 r^2 \left[ \cos\gamma Y_{20} + \frac{\sin\gamma}{\sqrt{2}} (Y_{22} + Y_{2,-2}) \right] + C \mathbf{l}\cdot\mathbf{s} + D \mathbf{l}^2\]

Where β represents the quadrupole deformation magnitude and γ is the triaxiality angle:

  • γ = 0°: Axial Prolate (cigar-shaped)
  • γ = 60°: Axial Oblate (pancake-shaped)
  • 0° < γ < 60°: Triaxial asymmetry (all axes unequal)

How to read the chart: Moving the Deformation slider in the sidebar shifts the vertical yellow indicator, showing how the levels cross and how shell gaps open and close at different shapes. The active triaxiality \(\gamma\) splits the levels further, which can be modified using the Triaxiality slider.

◉ Shell Correction δE_shell vs. Smearing Width γ

💡 Strutinsky Smearing & Plateau

The shell correction method extracts the quantum mechanical fluctuation energy by subtracting a smooth average energy \(\tilde{E}\) from the discrete sum of occupied energies:

\[\delta E_{\text{shell}} = \sum_{i=1}^{A} e_i - \tilde{E}(\gamma, p)\]

To calculate the smooth average energy, we smear the delta-function single-particle levels into smooth Gaussians multiplied by correcting Hermite polynomials of order \(p\). This creates a smoothed level density \(\tilde{g}(e)\):

\[\tilde{g}(e) = \frac{1}{\gamma\sqrt{\pi}} \sum_{i} e^{-u_i^2} f_p(u_i), \quad u_i = \frac{e - e_i}{\gamma}\]

For the shell correction to be physically meaningful, it must be independent of the arbitrary smearing parameter \(\gamma\). The chart on the left plots \(\delta E_{\text{shell}}\) vs. \(\gamma\).

The flat region where the curve becomes horizontal is the Stable Plateau Region (typically around \(\gamma \approx 1.2 \hbar\omega_0\)). In this region, \(\frac{\partial (\delta E)}{\partial \gamma} \approx 0\), yielding a robust quantum correction.

◉ Microscopic-Macroscopic Total Potential Energy Surface

💡 Potential Energy Surface Theory

In the microscopic-macroscopic model, the total deformation energy is computed as a 2D surface over the quadrupole deformation \(\beta\) and triaxiality \(\gamma\):

\[E_{\text{total}}(\beta, \gamma) = E_{\text{LDM, rel}}(\beta, \gamma) + \delta E_{\text{shell}, p}(\beta, \gamma) + \delta E_{\text{shell}, n}(\beta, \gamma)\]

The **PES** represents nuclear shapes using polar coordinates:

  • The radial distance from the origin is the deformation magnitude \(\beta\).
  • The polar angle is the triaxiality \(\gamma\). The wedge spans from \(0^\circ\) (prolate, football-shaped) to \(60^\circ\) (oblate, pancake-shaped).
  • Values in between (\(0^\circ < \gamma < 60^\circ\)) represent triaxial shapes.

The 3D surface plot on the left shows this energy surface. The **glowing marker** represents the current shape of the nucleus, and the **gold star** marks the predicted ground state. You can rotate and zoom the 3D view using your mouse.
